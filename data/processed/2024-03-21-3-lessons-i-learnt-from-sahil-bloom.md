**​  
3 things I learnt from Sahil Bloom**

Last week, I hosted Sahil Bloom on his first-ever visit to Delhi.   
We started with a breakfast information catchup with founders and creators.  
In the evening, we had a large gathering at the Bharat Mandapam with 1,000+ people.  
As I spent time with him, I observed 3 things about Sahil that stuck.

1. **Walk the talk**​  
   Sahil is one of the most disciplined people I have met.   
   He shares a lot of wisdom through social media — either through Twitter, Instagram, his newsletter, or through YouTube.  
   So much that one might question — does he believe in even half of the things he says?   
   He does!   
   And that, in my opinion, makes him so effective and believable.   
   Because he lives and breathes his own philosophy.   
   He walks the talk.   
   ​  
   Be it his workout routine, his cold plunge routine, his business discipline, his diet, his running, or his approach towards relationships.   
   I saw him living all that he says we should all do!   
   Talk is cheap. Action is expensive.  
   He pays that price every day.   
   ​
2. **Self-awareness is everything**​  
   In our discussion, it was obvious that Sahil truly knows who he is.  
   He knows he doesn't like to manage people.   
   He knows he loves vlogging.   
   He knows he isn't the best at sitting and speaking to the camera.   
   He knows he doesn't want to manage or operate businesses.   
   He knows his family is more important to him than anything else.   
   He knows he doesn't want the things he has to give up, because he sleeps every day at 8 pm.  
   ​  
   He knows himself.   
   And he has designed his life around that.   
   At 33, to have this clarity is enviable. Rare.   
   ​
3. **Being vulnerable is a strength**​  
   While sharing his reasons for moving back to NY (to be closer to his parents), Sahil broke down on stage. In front of 1,000+ people.   
   And not even for a second did he try to cover that up, pretend otherwise, or be comfortable.   
   He kept talking with tears flowing down his cheek.  
   At that moment, he wasn't weak.   
   He was strong af.   
   He was masculine af.   
   He was courageous.   
   A strong man isn't the one who never cries.   
   A strong man is the one who cries and still shows up to fight.  
   Sahil showed us that he is that man.   
   ​

The reason I love interacting with people is because every conversation teaches you something.   
I have recently started a series on YouTube called Money Matters, where I speak to normal people like you and I, to help them through their personal finance journey.   
This week, I had a chat with a school teacher, the sole earner of the family, bearing the responsibility of his family including ailing parents, in severe debt.   
And yet, I could see determination in his eyes. He could easily give up, but he chose not to.   
In the end, while we did come up with a plan, what left me touched was his conviction that within a year he would be out of this.   
​  
​**Everyone has a story to tell.   
Everyone has a lesson to share.  
Everyone has a difference to make.  
Everyone has a voice that can speak wisdom into the silence.  
If we are willing to listen.

Still reading **[The LEGO Story: How a Little Toy Sparked the World's Imagination](https://preview.convertkit-mail4.com/click/dpheh0hzhm/aHR0cHM6Ly9hbXpuLnRvLzRhYXZMT3A=)** by Jens Andersen.  
I am genuinely enjoying the book and realized I quite enjoy reading factual business stories.

While reading this, I also picked up **[The Tatas: How a Family Built a Business and a Nation](https://amzn.to/43spCuY)** by Girish Kuber.   
It is a mesmerizing account of how the Tata business came about. It is incredible if one begins to think of the family's contribution to not just business; but nation-building as well. From top institutes, hospitals, colleges, mega-sized businesses to establishing a global footprint — this kind of legacy is truly remarkable.   
If you like Tata as a brand, you will love this book. Very simple read.
