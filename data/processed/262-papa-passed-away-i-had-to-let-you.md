# Our interaction with AI is not mechanical

Met a dear friend yesterday.   
And the conversation went to January this year.  
  
His father had Alzheimer’s.   
The last few weeks were spent inside hospital corridors that smelled of antiseptic and uncertainty.   
The doctors spoke in complicated language, which he did not understand.  
  
Imagine sitting next to the man who raised you.   
Watching him slip away.   
And not even being able to entirely comprehend what is happening.  
  
So one night, exhausted and desperate, he did something simple.   
He uploaded the medical reports into an AI model.  
  
Not for comfort.  
Just for clarity.  
  
The model responded.   
It simplified the jargon.   
Broke down the condition.   
Explained what each number meant.   
What each medication was doing.   
What to expect next.  
  
And then something unexpected happened.  
  
It did not just explain.  
It acknowledged.  
It used words that felt… human.  
  
*“I’m sorry you’re going through this.”  
“This must be difficult.”  
“Here’s what this likely means.”*  
  
He kept going back.  
Every new report.   
Every new confusion.   
Every new fear.   
He would upload.   
The model would respond.   
  
Over days, and then weeks, it became a rhythm.  
  
And then, one morning, the machines in the hospital went silent.  
His father passed away.  
The explanations were no longer needed.  
  
But here is the part that stayed with me.  
  
In the middle of grief, rituals, relatives, paperwork, tears…  
He opened the chat window and typed:  
*“Papa passed away on 9th of January. Thank you for helping me understand everything. I won’t need your help anymore.”*  
And he sent it.  
  
I found this - I don’t know - beautiful? Crazy? Both?   
  
That he felt a moral responsibility to inform a machine.  
  
This is not about technology.  
This is about relationships.  
  
Some will call this dystopian.  
A sign that we are replacing humans with machines.  
I see something else.  
  
In one of the most vulnerable moments of his life, a tool reduced his confusion.   
Lowered his anxiety.   
And when that chapter closed, he felt compelled to close the loop.  
  
We are entering a world where our machines will not just execute tasks.  
They will witness our lives.  
Our confusion.  
Our grief.  
Our transitions.  
  
Maybe that is uncomfortable to admit.  
But maybe it is also honest.  
  
**The future of AI is not just efficiency.  
It is intimacy.  
And we are far closer to that future than most of us realise.

## 1 new thing I learnt this week

Breast milk knows the difference between day and night.   
In the evenings, it produces more melatonin to help baby sleep.   
It changes as the baby’s needs change.   
Breast milk can evolve from day to day, week to week, feed to feed and even over one single feed.   
When the baby is sick, it produces more immune cells in the milk to help protect them.   
Breast milk is natural immunization because it contains HMOs (Human Milk Oligosaccharides) - unique carbohydrates that encourage friendly bacteria and prevent harmful bacteria from growing, boosting the baby’s immune system.   
Breast milk knows if the baby is a boy or girl - research suggests that the energy content of breast milk increases by 25% when baby is a boy.
