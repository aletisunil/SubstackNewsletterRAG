**​  
Do you know which animal kills most humans every year?**

If you are thinking wild animals — lions, elephants, tigers — no.

If you are thinking snakes — good guess, but not the right one.

If you are thinking mosquitos — great guess. That's #2.

You know which animal kills the most humans every year?

Humans :)

WE kill more humans than any other animal every year.   
War, violence, fights, terrorism.

We somehow believe that the world is split into us and them.   
And we feel that for someone to win, someone else has to lose.   
That's what we have been fed every day.   
That the world is finite — its wealth, growth, and opportunities.   
And the only way one gets wealth, growth or opportunity is to snatch it away from someone else.

**This is called a zero-sum mindset.** ​  
A mindset that says:  
If someone has to become +1, then someone will have to become -1 so that the sum is always zero.   
If one goes up, then one has to go down.

There is an alternative to this mindset.   
​**It's called the positive-sum mindset.** ​  
A mindset that says:  
If someone becomes +1, someone else can also become +1 so that the sum is positive.   
If one goes up, then another one can also go up.

This mindset believes that there is space for everyone.   
There is wealth for everyone, growth for everyone, and opportunities for everyone.   
I am not competing with anyone.   
Instead, I am finding my own space in the world.   
A world where there is space for everyone.

When you begin to see the world as an ally, or at best, a witness to your journey as against competition, 3 things happen:

1. You liberate yourself from all the fears and insecurities of you being good enough or not.   
   Everyone has their own journey — so the question of good enough isn't about you or someone else.   
   It is about you and who you were yesterday.
2. You begin to appreciate the journey of others instead of envying the journey of others.   
   "If they can do it, so can I".
3. You do not live in FOMO.  
   You aren't perpetually thinking about what you aren't doing that someone else is, and living in fear of them winning and you losing.

**There are two ways to live life.  
One is to see everyone's success with suspicion.  
One is to see everyone's success with inspiration.

Started reading "**[What I learnt about investing from Darwin](https://amzn.to/4bVzroG)**" by Pulak Prasad.  
Pulak is the founder of Nalanda Capital, one of the most successful investing firms in India.

It is quite a fascinating read for the evolved investor (not recommended for early investors) because it compares two seemingly disconnected fields to show how everything is ultimately connected!

​

Observations:

* The younger you are, the more money you think you need — not because you need that money, but because you have little sense of how much money is actually needed to live life.   
  For example: Those below 18 think they need above 50Cr, while those about 40 think they need between 3-10Cr. Both can't be true. And I would any day go with the judgment of those in their 40s.
* Almost everyone agrees that less than 1Cr will not cut it!
* The sweet spot is 1-10Cr.

**My answer:**​  
Between 3-10Cr is MORE than enough to live one's life; if managed smartly.
