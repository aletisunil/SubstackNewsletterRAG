# Comparison is a terrible thing

When young, comparison is a daily activity.   
Everyone seems to be winning, and somehow, it makes us feel as if we are losing.   
Which is so weird, if you think about it.   
Someone else’s life.   
Someone else’s upbringing.   
Someone else’s circumstances.   
Yet somehow, their outcomes make us question ours.   
  
Here is why:  
We have been raised on the notion that the world is a zero-sum game.   
That the sum of everyone’s gains and losses will always add up to zero.   
Which means if someone is +2, then someone will have to be -2, for the world to be in order.

In other words:   
**For someone to win, someone has to lose.**

So when we see someone winning, it psychologically tells us that we must be losing somewhere.   
Especially if that someone is known to us.   
Our batchmate, our colleague, our relative.   
Because the world feels even more zero-sum in close proximity.

However, the truth of the world is that the world is NOT a zero-sum game.   
It is a positive-sum game.

In other words, **In the world, NO ONE has to lose for someone to win.   
There is space for everyone to win.** 

Everyone can own a car.   
Everyone can own a house.   
Everyone can be rich.   
Wealth/money is not limited, which needs to be distributed.   
It is created through value, and thus, can be created by anyone.

What does this mean for you?  
If you see someone winning, you have two choices from now on.

Choice 1:   
Feel terrible about your life, your choices, your situations.   
And plead in front of God - why, God, why - why me?   
or…  
  
Choice 2:  
Realize that them winning has no bearing at all on your winning.   
They are winning - good for them.   
You can win too.   
You have to figure out what is needed for you to win.   
And go ahead and do that.

What is your choice going to be?

**Comparing your life with others is the worst use of your time.**

## 1 new thing I learnt this week

Why is a full marathon 42 kms? That seems like a weird, arbitrary distance.

Originally, the race distance varied.   
  
The now-standard 42.195 km (26.2 miles) was fixed after the 1908 London Olympics, when the course was adjusted so the race could start at Windsor Castle and finish in front of the royal box at the stadium.   
  
That version stuck and became official in 1921.
