# How rich are you?

Someone asked on Twitter:  
”*tell me how rich u are without actually telling me.”*My response to this:

1. As a family, we travel for 100+ days a year.  
2. At 45, my metabolic age is 36.  
3. I work from home and so does my entire team.  
4. Our kids love spending time with us.  
5. I love spending time with myself - reading, writing, learning, and thinking.   
I am my best friend.

In my 20s, I chased money as a goal.   
Then when I did not have any of it, no matter how hard I tried, I began to hate it.   
Now when I have a lot of it, I realize that money was never the goal.   
  
It was simply the means.   
To the eventual goal.   
  
The eventual goal of living your life the way you want.   
Having the freedom to make your choices.   
  
The choice of whether to build a unicorn, bootstrap, or not work at all.   
The choice of whether to travel or buy a car instead.   
The choice of whether to have lots of kids or none at all.   
The choice of whether to stay in a city or move to a farm.

The role of money is not to buy you things.   
The role of money is to give you freedom.   
To make whichever choices you want to make in life.

Dropped reading **[Politics On the Edge](https://amzn.to/475wJwN)** by Rory Stewart. The book started off interesting, but I couldn’t relate to it beyond a point. The British Political commentary wasn’t as captivating.

Started reading **[Half - Lion: How P.V. Narasimha Rao Transformed India](https://amzn.to/4hApKiU)** by Vinay Sitapati. Written by the same author who wrote Jugalbandi (the story of LK Advani and AB Vajpayee), this focuses on the period when Rao became the PM during a rather difficult time for the country. He is also credited with ushering in the era of liberalization in 1991, with Manmohan Singh as the Finance Minister.   
  
I must compliment Vinay Sitapati for his storytelling - it is gripping, objective, deep and allows the reader to imagine the sequence as if unfolding in front of them.   
Quite a joyful read.
