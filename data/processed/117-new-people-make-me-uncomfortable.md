# Self-doubt

Someone sent an email early this week:   
  
*Until now, the job was fully remote, which suited me well.   
However, we’ve recently shifted to a hybrid model, requiring us to be in the office three days a week.   
**I’ve realized that being around new people makes me quite uncomfortable.**  
I become overly conscious, avoid conversations, and tend to keep to myself.*

*The second issue I’m facing is that my seniors have started treating me like a pushover when assigning unnecessary deadlines and putting me under pressure, even when there’s no urgency.*   
*I’ve seen this pattern before, and I want to break it.   
**How can I stand up for myself without damaging my work relationships?***

I asked her 2 questions:

1. Why do you get self-conscious around people? What are you telling yourself at that moment?
2. When your senior is being unreasonable, why do you not push back? What are you telling yourself at that moment?

She replied:

To Q1:

* *I believe I am an introverted person and have always been that way.*
* *I often view myself as inferior to others in social or official gatherings. I think I’ve always struggled with low confidence since childhood, and unfortunately, I never really worked on improving it.*

To Q2:

* *The way some seniors speak to me, often with a very authoritative tone, makes me feel disheartened.*
* *I think a part of me holds back out of respect.*
* *I’ve always been someone who prefers to stay focused on my work rather than get into arguments or conflicts.*

She was wonderfully honest in her responses. And I owed it to her to be honest too.

**Here was my response**:

You’re not broken.   
But you are stuck in a story that no longer serves you.

You call yourself an introvert.   
But introversion isn’t the issue here.   
Avoidance is.   
You’re not avoiding conversations because you prefer solitude - you're avoiding them because you're afraid of not measuring up.   
**That’s not introversion. That’s self-doubt disguised as personality.**

But here’s the truth: nobody ever learned how to swim by standing on the shore and watching others talk about the ocean.   
You’re in the room now.   
That’s the ocean.   
Jump in.   
You’ll learn by showing up as you are, not by waiting till you become "enough."

Your childhood experiences might have convinced you that staying quiet is safer.   
Maybe it once was.   
**But now, silence is the very thing keeping you small.**

Now to the workplace.   
You say you don’t push back because you don’t want to be disrespectful or argumentative.   
Being assertive is not being disrespectful.   
**And being silent is not being respectful. It’s being invisible.**

You are not helping your future self by avoiding temporary discomfort today.   
You’re just deferring the cost.   
It will show up later - as resentment, burnout, or being taken for granted.

Here’s what I would do:

* Pick one moment this week to say something you normally wouldn’t — in a meeting, a conversation, or even a casual chat. Start small. But start.
* Script your pushback: “I understand the urgency, but I’ll need until X to do this well.” - Firm. Respectful. Clear.
* Stop apologizing for not knowing enough. Nobody knows everything. You’re allowed to ask. You’re allowed to not have been everywhere. You’re allowed to learn in real time.

You are clearly getting in your own way.

**This version of you - the quiet one, the one who makes herself smaller - is a story that needs to stop.   
Time to write a better one.**
