**MY 5TH ILLUSTRATED BOOK IS OUT - MISTAKES I MADE WITH MY MONEY**

---

We are often told that chasing money will not make us happy. That is false. It is the classic self-help gyaan that sounds so good that you would want to believe it, but doesn't come with any practicality attached to it.

Yes, it is true that chasing money (beyond a point) doesn't make us happy. BUT, one has to first reach to that point. The truth is, most of us haven't yet reached that point.

Our parents have struggled all their lives to make ends meet, they gave us love, food, upbringing, shelter and sacrificed a lot of their own desires to do so. For them, survival was more important than anything else. Any bad mistake in life could be life-altering.

So most of us, I assume, grew up with just enough, perhaps even lesser.

And every additional money coming in, DID MAKE A DIFFERENCE. It did make life easier. It did make us more comfortable.

Research backs this up. In developed economies such as the US, beyond $70,000 per year, there is NO significant increase in a person's happiness, with increase in money.

For developing economies such as India - that is NOT TRUE. There is a visible increase in happiness, with increasing income, because let's face it, we are a poor country. So having money gives us access to better things in life - healthcare, education, shelter, living conditions.

​

I share this because for the longest time in my life I dismissed money. I hated money because I felt that money was the core reason for our family's miseries.

And that was a mistake.

**HOW I INVEST MY MONEY**

---

Last year, when I stepped down as the CEO of my startup (nearbuy), I had no money. Yeah, I had paper wealth - because I had investments in startups and owned equity in nearbuy too. But none of that resulted in actual cash (one of the mistakes I made with my money - not optimizing for cash).

So I had to create a revenue stream again. That took time and thankfully today I do have one. To the extent that I can start investing my money and not make the same mistakes that I made previously.

**RESULTS OF LAST WEEK'S POLL**

---

Last week I asked all of you

What would you choose and why? You have to pick one.

1. Help a mother begging for money for her young child, who hasn't eaten in a day.
2. Help a son begging for money for his old mother who is quite ill and needs medicines.

OH MY GOD! The responses were crazy. Almost equally split. Option 1 would have won marginally.

It was the reactions and explanations that blew my mind.

People were analyzing this as if it were a math problem in the CAT exam!   
​ *"What is the age of the child/old mother?"   
"Is the mother/son physically abled?"   
"Who has a higher probability of dying?"   
"What are they wearing?"*

Really?

Real life mein itna sochne ka mauka milta hai? (Does real life give you the opportunity to analyze to this extent?)

A LOT of you outright objected to the concept of begging. Which I always find fascinating. We have been trained to believe that begging is bad. And if you are physically abled then you should not beg.

I do not believe in the idealogy. For me, begging is just another form of product or service being offered. Most of us offer help to beggers because it makes us feel good. The act of offering help to beggars legitimizes feeling good about oneself.

But it is our choice not to. Just as it is our choice to buy a Honda and not a Maruti, or a Bajaj and not a Hero.

And then there were a lot who said - I will help both.   
Ofcourse, Sherlock - you will help both. We are not devils that we are choosing one of them to help :)   
The point is - to pick and we explain.

So here was the outcome.

Most people will help the mother begging for her child because they felt that the child has a better future ahead, as against the old mother who has perhaps lived her life already.

What I do know is that I clouded the judgement of almost all of you by using the word beg.

Had I used the word help - things would have been different.

Play of words.
