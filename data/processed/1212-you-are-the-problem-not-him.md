# A brother trying to save his brother

I got an email last week:  
  
*This email is about me not being able to pave a path for my younger brother.   
He’s 31, freelancing as a photographer (he’s good at it), and spends most of his time trading which hasn’t helped him gain anything. We’ve been trying to get him to go back to college (he didn’t complete his undergrad) or get him some job, but none of that has worked out.*

*Otherwise, he’s a great and sincere kid, extremely fit, totally responsible, and very disciplined.*

*Can you suggest a way forward for him?*

My first response was:  
*Who is funding his life?*

His response:  
*He does some freelance work as a photographer whenever he gets an assignment. But majorly, it’s me and my mother supporting him.*

Which is when the problem became clear.   
My response was:  
*Then, you and your mother are the problem. Throw him at the deep end of the pool and he will have no choice but to learn how to swim.*

The reality is, almost all of us have a very twisted sense of what help is.   
  
Help is giving directions.   
Help is listening.   
Help is offering guidance.   
  
But the biggest definition of help is - help is not your decision to make.   
Instead, help is something that someone asks for.

If you help someone without their permission, you don’t help them.   
  
You make them dependent.   
You become their crutch.   
To the point where they forget how to walk on their own.

That’s what happened here as well.   
The brother thinks he is trying to help his brother by supporting him financially.   
But by doing so, he is never going to let his brother walk on his own.

His brother will never realise how courageous he is.   
Because he has never had to fight, or to be courageous.   
Because his brother and mother have been fighting the battles for him until now.

**We do not realise how courageous we are, until being courageous is the only option we have.**

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mVwt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F75371124-ef84-48be-bef6-c28f496a004d_818x1047.png)](https://www.justinmath.com/files/the-math-academy-way.pdf)

Still reading **[The Math Academy Way](https://www.justinmath.com/files/the-math-academy-way.pdf)** by Justin Skycak.   
Justin is the founder of The Math Academy, which is an AI-enabled math learning platform for kids.

I have reached the section that hit me the most, and challenged my current understanding:  
  
The notion that people do not have different learning methods. They just have different learning speeds. And that’s a function of something called WMC - Working Memory Capacity.   
So, a system that attempts to teach has to be able to determine the WMC of a student and change the pace of teaching accordingly.   
Which is why classroom setups fail - because everyone is expected to have the same pace of learning.   
  
This is why 1:1 teaching remains the most effective form of learning, because the process is customised to the student’s pace (or WMC).

## 1 new thing I learnt this week

The word maverick comes from a real person  
Samuel Augustus Maverick (1803–1870), a Texas lawyer, land baron and rancher.

He became famous because:

1. He refused to brand his cattle, which was unheard of in mid-1800s Texas.
2. When unbranded cattle were found roaming freely, people said, “Those are *Maverick’s*.”
3. Over time, maverick shifted from meaning *unbranded cattle* to meaning *a person who does not conform*.
