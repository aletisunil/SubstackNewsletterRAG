# 5 lifestyle decisions that transformed my health completely

**1. High-protein, high-carb, low-fat diet**   
  
3 years back, I sat down to critically break down the macros of my average diet.   
It comprised 40% carbs, 40% fat, and 20% protein.   
I flipped the ratio.   
  
Today I have 40% protein, 40% carbs, and 20% fat.   
Being a vegetarian (who eats eggs), I get my daily protein from egg white, tofu, high-protein low-fat paneer, dal, and whey.   
  
**2. Two meals a day** 

We are so used to the concept of breakfast, lunch, and dinner that I certainly didn’t question it for the longest time.   
  
If I were to be honest with myself, the 3 meals were entertainment eating. Not necessity eating.   
  
So I took out one meal.   
Today, I have my first meal of the day at 11 am and the second (and last) meal at 4 pm.   
  
**3. Sleeping and waking up at the same time.** 

Our body loves a routine.   
We mess it up by playing roulette with it every day.   
Especially when it comes to our sleep.   
Nearly a decade back, disturbed by the randomness of my sleeping and waking hours, I decided to fix the schedule.   
  
Today, I sleep between 9-9:30 pm and wake up between 4:30-5 am every day.   
  
This means saying no to a lot of social events and dinners, which frankly, I am happy to :)

**4. Meditation**

I have been meditating for nearly a decade now.   
Started with guided meditation on Headspace, every day for 10 mins.   
Max 100-150 days a year.   
I saw benefits, but I didn’t experience the consistency I desired.   
  
After 3 years, I moved to non-guided meditation on Calm App (not promoted).   
30 mins every day, to the sound of water, birds and air.   
  
I haven’t missed a day in 6.5 years!   
  
I’d like to believe it is the reason I live in the present, have a resting heart rate of 47-48bpm, and rarely have high stress.

**5. Lifting weights** 

13 years back, when I started working out, I stuck to the treadmill like most others.   
It was when I started to read more, that the benefits of lifting weights dawned upon me.   
  
Today, I lift weights 6 days a week, have body fat of 18-19% and a VO2 max of 50+, which places me in the top percentile of men in my age and weight range.   
  
It doesn’t hurt that I also look good in the mirror :)

In addition to this, post-COVID, we also started eating significantly organic food as a family.   
  
- We grow our own vegetables (have rented a farm - and eat reasonable veggies only grown completely organic)  
- We buy organic products only (pulses, ghee, milk, etc.)   
- Almost zero packaged food  
- Virtually no food ordering  
  
All of this costs a fair bit of money, and we recognize our privilege in being able to exercise these choices.   
  
Finally,   
  
6 months back, I bought a Whoop (not promoted), and that’s genuinely helped me track so many of my vitals.   
  
It is a significant investment for someone residing in India, but I’d highly recommend it if you can afford one.   
  
It’s helped me remain accountable to myself and also experiment with my patterns to see the impact.   
  
For example,   
- On days that I eat late, my RHR shoots up to 53-54   
- On days I sleep late, my HRV drops down to 35  
- If I don’t lift weights for a week, my VO2 max drops below 48  
- If I see my phone right before sleeping, my restorative sleep duration drops

**The real reason**  
For 32 years, I treated my body carelessly.  
Like a dumping ground.  
Like something endlessly stretchable.  
Like something that would always adjust.  
  
That illusion broke when I was diagnosed with avascular necrosis.  
A hip surgery followed.  
3 months on bed rest.  
5 months on crutches.  
  
That experience made one thing painfully clear.  
The body is the vehicle of life.  
And it cannot be taken for granted.  
  
The last 12 years have been about two things.  
- Making peace with my past self  
- Preparing responsibly for my future self  
  
Doing what is in my control.  
Mentally.  
Physically.

## 1 new thing I learnt this week

Your nose is ALWAYS in your line of sight, but your brain ignores it.

What is actually happening:

* Your nose is physically always in your visual field.
* Light from your nose continuously hits your retina.
* Your brain actively filters it out through a process called perceptual suppression.
